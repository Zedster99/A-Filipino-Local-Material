What is Buntal?

Buntal is a natural and organic fiber from large leaves of abundant palm trees, specifically the Buri Palm Trees, that are grown sustainably in the Philippines. This tree consists of the Corypha palm frond (leaf) is fan shaped, while the petiole are the leaflets (segments) extending from the central point at the end of the frond stalk, which are beaten until the fibers separate—drawn out by hand.

The Preparation of the Buntal Fibers

The buntal is laid out to dry in the sun until it manifests a lovely warm-white color. This exhibits a much lighter color in comparison with another local material—the straw— which leads to confusion in the usage of materials in determining which is which. Next, the dried fibers are smashed again to soften the strands, meeting the standard for weaving. Commonly, these strands are also dyed for a more stylish and vibrant product.

Process

How to Make a Fashionable Buntal Hat

Step 1: Prepare a round mat of buntal (your choice of color), sold in markets, together with equipment such as round crown hat block, water sprayer, and steam iron.

Step 2: Damp the mat with a couple pumps from the water sprayer, then use a drafting pin to align the center of the mat’s weaving pattern with the crown block.

Step 3: Pull the mat down smoothly over the crown of the block and secure with a blocking elastic and several pins.

Step 4: Apply three coats of stiffener to the crown of the hat and use a yellow wax pencil to mark where to cut the brim.

Step 5: Considering that the fibers are quite thin, the next step is to stay-stitch about 1/4” (3mm) to the inside of the line on which you want to cut down your brim.

Step 6: If you want to adjust the curvature of the brim, adjust the tension of the stay-stitch before cutting down the brim edge. White glue can be applied to the cut fibers to polish the look.

Step 7: Add your own final touches.

Step 8: Enjoy the finished product!

History

Originally, buntal products were primarily produced in the towns of Sariaya and Tayabas, due to the buri palm plantations in Quezon Province. The industry expanded into Baliuag, Bulacan between 1907 to 1909, originally introduced by Mariano Deveza who originally hailed from Lucban, Quezon. Introducing buntal products weaving to Baliuag was easy since the town already had a preexisting weaving industry producing textiles, pañuelos (kerchiefs), tapis, as well as woven hats made from bamboo straw. Buntal products production in Baliuag was further enhanced by Dolores Maniquis who softened the fibers through the use of a wooden roller known as an iluhan. In the year 1923, Chinese producers from Hong Kong hired Buntal weavers from the Philippines. Having acquired the weaving process, they then began to mass produce. This affected the local industry to such an extent that Representative Antonio Villarama tried to pass a bill to ban the export of buntal fiber (though it failed). Because of competition from Chinese factories, the buntal industry waned until the 1960s when it was again revived by the Villones family from Sariaya, Quezon. Their products were mostly sourced from suppliers from Baliuag, although they also bought products from nearby towns in Bulacan, Pampanga, and Nueva Ecija. Their products were primarily exported to the United States, Mexico, and Italy. The family accounted for around a quarter of the total monthly production of buntal hats in the 1960s. The industry, however, is once again in danger of extinction due to the growing lack of skilled workers and the declining demand. Most remaining weavers of buntal are elderly. The government of Baliuag has been taking measures to promote buntal products to the local market. In 2011, Rosario Quizon-Decasa, one of the last remaining weavers in Baliuag, also started a project to teach buntal weaving skills to inmates in the Bulacan Provincial Jail, due to the lack of interest among youths in Bulacan.
